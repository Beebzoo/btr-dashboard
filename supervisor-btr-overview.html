What is the Bachelor Thesis Research?

Understanding the BTR program and what it means for MSP students

BTR Overview

The Bachelor Thesis Research (BTR) is the capstone research experience for students in the Maastricht Science Programme (MSP). It is a comprehensive, independent research project that marks the culmination of their undergraduate scientific training.

Key Characteristics

  • Full-time commitment: Students dedicate an entire semester to their BTR
  • Independent research: Students formulate their own research plans and interpret results
  • Complete scientific cycle: From problem identification through experimentation to reporting
  • Flexible location: Can be conducted at MSP, other UM locations, or external institutions
  • Final requirement: Must be completed to graduate from MSP

Start Times

BTRs typically begin at the start of semesters:

  • September Cohort: Starts September 1, ends January 30
  • February Cohort: Starts February 2, ends June 30

BTR Aims & Learning Objectives

The BTR is designed to prepare students for research careers and advanced study by helping them:

Research Skills Development

  • Perform high-level academic research independently
  • Gain insight into the complete process of scientific research
  • Gather and apply independent research skills while working in a research team
  • Understand how to design experiments and interpret results

Communication & Documentation

  • Write a comprehensive research proposal with clear aims and methodology
  • Write and defend a complete research thesis
  • Communicate complex scientific issues to non-scientists
  • Present research findings to scientific peers

Specialization & Career Preparation

  • Further specialize in one or more areas of natural sciences
  • Prepare for admission to research master's programs
  • Develop critical thinking and problem-solving abilities
  • Experience real-world research challenges and methodologies

üéØ Independence is Key

The BTR places a particular premium on independence. While thesis topics may be part of a supervisor's ongoing research program, students are expected to:

  • Formulate their own research plan
  • Carry out research as an independent team member
  • Interpret results themselves in context of scientific literature
  • Take ownership of their project and outcomes

Student Eligibility & Requirements

Academic Requirements

To begin their BTR, students must have:

  • Minimum 145 ECTS credits completed (of the 150 non-BTR credits)
  • Satisfied any prerequisite course requirements
  • Identified a research supervisor with a PhD
  • Secured approval to start from the BTR Office

Student Level & Background

BTR students are final-year bachelor students who have completed three years of intensive scientific training in the Maastricht Science Programme, including:

  • Core science courses across biology, chemistry, physics, and mathematics
  • Problem-Based Learning (PBL) experience
  • Research skills courses and laboratory training
  • Academic writing and critical thinking development

‚úÖ What This Means for Supervisors

Students arrive with solid foundational knowledge and research skills, but they are still learning. They will need:

  • Introduction to specialized research techniques
  • Guidance on experimental design in your specific field
  • Support with results interpretation and scientific writing
  • Regular feedback and mentorship throughout the semester

Where BTRs Can Take Place

Students have flexibility in choosing their research location:

1. Within Maastricht University

  • MSP faculty research groups
  • Other UM faculties (e.g., FASoS, FHML, Law, Psychology)
  • UM-affiliated research institutes

2. Other Netherlands Institutions

  • Other Dutch universities
  • Research institutes (e.g., TNO, RIVM)
  • Private research facilities

3. International Institutions

  • Universities abroad
  • International research centers
  • Company research departments

What Qualifies as BTR Research?

The BTR must be a genuine research project that follows the complete scientific cycle. Not all work in a research environment qualifies as a BTR.

‚úÖ Acceptable BTR Research

A project qualifies as BTR research if it includes:

  • Clear research questions: Specific, testable scientific questions
  • Testable hypotheses: Predictions based on scientific reasoning
  • Independent research: Student-driven investigation (not routine tasks)
  • Scientific methodology: Appropriate experimental or analytical approaches
  • Data collection & analysis: Gathering and interpreting original data
  • Original contribution: New knowledge or insights, however modest
  • Scientific reporting: Full documentation in thesis format

‚ùå NOT Acceptable as BTR

The following do NOT qualify as BTR research:

  • Routine laboratory tasks or service work
  • General internships without research component
  • Purely technical work for host institution
  • Projects without clear research questions or hypotheses
  • Work that doesn't result in interpretable scientific findings

üí° If You're Unsure

If you're uncertain whether a proposed project qualifies as appropriate BTR research, contact the BTR Office early in the planning stage. They can provide guidance and ensure the project meets academic standards.

The Five BTR Components

The BTR is assessed through five distinct components, each evaluating different aspects of the student's research abilities:

Component Weight When What It Assesses
Research Proposal 15% Week 4 Literature review, aims, methodology, planning
Midpoint Practical 15% Mid-semester Early research skills, independence, problem-solving
Written Thesis 25% Week ~19 Complete research documentation and scientific writing
Final Practical 35% End of semester Overall research execution and group presentation
BTR Video 10% After thesis Science communication to non-specialists (5-7 min)

Passing Requirements

Students must achieve:

  • Weighted average of 5.5 or higher across all components
  • Individual components may be below 5.5 if compensated by other grades
  • Dutch grading scale: 1.0 (lowest) to 10.0 (highest)

Who's Involved in the BTR?

The BTR involves several key people who each play distinct roles in supporting and assessing the student:

1. Research Supervisor (You)

  • Qualifications: Must have PhD; typically has substantial research experience
  • Primary role: Provide research guidance, feedback, and assessment
  • Grading responsibility: Grade proposal (50%), midpoint practical (100%), thesis (50%), final practical (100%)
  • Day-to-day supervision: May be delegated to lab members, but you retain final responsibility

2. Internal Advisor

  • Who they are: Usually MSP or allied staff member
  • Assigned by: BTR Coordinator (student cannot choose)
  • Role: Verify scientific quality and academic standards
  • NOT an expert: Not necessarily familiar with your research area
  • NOT a co-supervisor: Does not provide feedback on drafts or contribute to research design
  • Grading responsibility: Grade proposal (50%), thesis (50%), video (50%)
  • Student meetings: Discusses progress, work ethic, and integration with student

3. BTR Coordinator (Dr. Panos Christakoglou)

  • Oversees overall BTR program quality
  • Handles special circumstances, extensions, and disputes
  • Makes final decisions on BTR matters
  • Awards final BTR grades officially

4. BTR Administrator (Martijn Jeurissen)

  • Manages day-to-day BTR administration
  • Primary contact for procedural questions
  • Coordinates grade collection and documentation
  • Assists with Collent platform and technical issues

ü§ù Collaboration Between Supervisor and Internal Advisor

While you and the Internal Advisor grade independently, the BTR Office may ask you to discuss if your grades differ significantly (>1.5 points). This ensures:

  • Understanding of different perspectives
  • Consistency in academic standards
  • Fair assessment for students

Grades may or may not change after discussion, but communication is essential.

Additional Modules During BTR

Students may request to take one additional module (maximum 5 ECTS) alongside their BTR if working in or near Maastricht.

Your Position

‚ö†Ô∏è You Are NOT Obligated to Approve

Key points about additional module requests:

  • Your approval required: Along with Board of Examiners approval
  • Must be requested before BTR starts: Not after BTR has begun
  • Student must consult you: Discuss how they'll manage extra workload
  • Only for local BTRs: Not allowed for BTRs abroad or outside Maastricht
  • If you refuse: Module must be completed after BTR ends
  • If requested late: Module must be completed after BTR ends

Consider Before Approving

  • Will the student be able to maintain full-time commitment to BTR research?
  • Will the additional module conflict with research hours or meetings?
  • Does the student have a realistic plan for managing both commitments?
