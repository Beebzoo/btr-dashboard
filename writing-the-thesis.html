BTR Written Thesis Guidelines

Your comprehensive guide to writing your final thesis

üìñ Overview

Your written thesis is the fullest account of your BTR work, presenting your results, conclusions, and personal reflection. This document constitutes 25% of your overall BTR grade and is assessed by both your Research Supervisor and Internal Advisor.

You can begin writing immediately after receiving proposal feedback, and many sections can be written as your work progresses. Use this guide to understand what's expected and how to produce a high-quality academic thesis.

Key Requirements at a Glance

  • üìÖ Deadline: Five weeks before graduation (check your personalized calendar)
  • üìä Weight: 25% of total BTR grade
  • üìÑ Word Count: 8,000-15,000 words (excluding references and appendices)
  • üìé Upload Limit: 25MB on Collent
  • ‚úì Draft Review: One draft review allowed with Research Supervisor
  • ‚úì Citation Style: Flexible (APA standard at MSP, but consult supervisor)
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Original Work Only: Plagiarism = automatic fail

üìÖ Section 1: Timeline & Submission Deadline

‚ñº

When Can I Start Writing?

You can begin writing your thesis directly after receiving feedback on your proposal. Many parts can be written as your work progresses:

  • Introduction: Based on your proposal (revised according to feedback)
  • Methods/Experimental Section: Write as you conduct your research
  • Results: Document as data is collected
  • Analysis & Discussion: Typically completed in final weeks
  • Critical Reflection: Write during final weeks

Deadline

The written thesis is due five weeks before graduation.

Your exact deadline is provided in the personalized calendar you received at the start of your BTR.

‚ö†Ô∏è Plan Accordingly: Set aside several weeks at the end of your research for:
  • Analysis of results
  • Writing your discussion and conclusions
  • Completing your critical reflection
  • Proofreading and formatting
  • Final submission preparations

üìù Section 5: Abstract

‚ñº

What is an Abstract?

The abstract provides a brief but complete overview of your entire work. It should be placed at the start of your thesis (after title page, before main text).

Requirements

Maximum Length: 200 words

Must Include:

  • Brief background/context
  • Your research question or aim
  • Methods used (very briefly)
  • Key results
  • Main conclusions
  • Significance/implications

Should NOT Include:

  • Citations/references
  • Detailed methodology
  • Extensive background information
  • Tables or figures

Purpose

The abstract allows readers to quickly understand:

  • What you studied
  • How you studied it
  • What you found
  • Why it matters

Someone reading only your abstract should understand the essence of your entire thesis.

Writing Tips:
  • ‚úì Write your abstract last (after completing all other sections)
  • ‚úì Be concise but comprehensive
  • ‚úì Use clear, direct language
  • ‚úì Avoid jargon where possible
  • ‚úì Make every word count ‚Äì you only have 200!

üìö Section 6: Introduction

‚ñº

Purpose of the Introduction

Your introduction provides:

  • Background to your research
  • Rationale for why this work is important
  • Overview of relevant theoretical concepts
  • Context within the broader field
  • Your specific research questions/hypotheses

Building from Your Proposal

Good News: Your introduction will likely be based on your research proposal.
  • ‚úì This is NOT plagiarism ‚Äì you are allowed to copy parts of your proposal for your introduction
  • ‚úì Revise and improve based on feedback received on your proposal
  • ‚úì Expand sections that were too brief in the proposal
  • ‚úì Update with any relevant literature published since your proposal

Required Elements

1. Broader Context ("The Story So Far")
  • What has been done in this research area?
  • What is the current state of knowledge?
  • Why is this topic important?
2. Knowledge Gaps
  • Where are the gaps in our current understanding?
  • What questions remain unanswered?
  • Why does this gap matter?
3. Your Research Focus
  • How does your study address these gaps?
  • What is your specific contribution?
  • Your hypotheses or research questions
4. Wider Significance
  • Potential impact of your work
  • How it enhances knowledge in the field
  • Broader implications (if applicable)

Citation Requirements

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Rule: Do NOT simply cite references you've only read about in other papers
  • You must have read the original sources yourself
  • Citing papers you haven't read will result in downgrading
  • If you found a reference in another paper, go read the original before citing it

Citation Style:

  • Flexible (unlike the proposal which required numbered citations)
  • APA is MSP standard, but any consistent format is acceptable
  • Consult your supervisor for their preference (numbered citations still common in many fields)

Structure Tips

  • ‚úì Start broad, then narrow to your specific topic
  • ‚úì Use clear topic sentences for each paragraph
  • ‚úì Create logical flow between ideas
  • ‚úì Reference all claims with proper citations
  • ‚úì End with clear statement of your research aims/questions

üî¨ Section 7: Methods/Experimental Section

‚ñº

Purpose

The methods section describes exactly how you conducted your research. Another scientist should be able to precisely replicate your work based on your description.

What to Include

Level of Detail Required:

Materials:

  • All equipment used (with model numbers/specifications if relevant)
  • Chemicals, reagents, or biological materials
  • Software and versions
  • Special preparations or treatments

Procedures:

  • Step-by-step description of what you did
  • Order of operations
  • Timing and conditions (temperature, pH, concentrations, etc.)
  • Sample sizes and replication
  • Controls used
  • Any deviations from standard protocols

Statistical Analysis:

  • Software used for analysis
  • Statistical tests performed
  • Significance levels
  • How data was processed

Participants/Subjects (if applicable):

  • Number and characteristics
  • Recruitment methods
  • Ethical approval details
  • Inclusion/exclusion criteria

Writing Style

  • Use past tense (you're describing what you did)
  • Be specific and precise ("heated to 65¬∞C for 2 hours" not "heated briefly")
  • Include enough detail but avoid unnecessary narrative
  • Use subheadings to organize complex methods

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ‚ùå Being too vague ("samples were prepared appropriately")
  • ‚ùå Assuming the reader knows standard procedures in your field (explain anyway)
  • ‚ùå Omitting important details about conditions or parameters
  • ‚ùå Writing in first person narrative ("First, I took a sample...")
If You're Unsure:

Discuss with your supervisor:

  • What level of detail is appropriate for your field
  • Whether certain standard procedures need full description
  • Field-specific conventions for methods sections

Golden Rule: When in doubt, include more detail rather than less. Your assessors should never have to guess what you did.

üìä Section 8: Results

‚ñº

Purpose

The results section presents what you found without interpretation. This is where you show your data objectively.

What to Include

Types of Results Presentation:

1. Figures

  • Graphs showing trends, patterns, or relationships
  • Clear labels on all axes
  • Legends explaining symbols/colors
  • Captions explaining what is shown
  • Referenced in text ("Figure 1 shows...")

2. Tables

  • Organized data in rows and columns
  • Clear column headers
  • Units clearly indicated
  • Captions describing table contents
  • Referenced in text ("Table 2 presents...")

3. Text Descriptions

  • Always necessary in addition to figures/tables
  • Guide readers through your results
  • Highlight key findings
  • Direct attention to important features

4. Statistical Analysis

  • Report relevant statistics (p-values, confidence intervals, etc.)
  • Show results of statistical tests
  • Indicate significance levels
  • Follow conventions for your field

Organization

Logical Structure:

  • Present results in order that answers your research questions
  • Group related findings together
  • Use subheadings to organize different experiments or analyses
  • Flow should be clear and easy to follow

What NOT to Include:

  • ‚ùå Interpretation of results (save for Discussion)
  • ‚ùå Raw data (unless specifically requested ‚Äì use appendix)
  • ‚ùå Failed experiments (unless relevant to understanding your final approach)

Quality Standards

Figures & Tables:

  • High resolution and clearly readable
  • Professional appearance
  • Consistent formatting throughout
  • Appropriately sized (not too small or overwhelming)
  • Compressed to meet file size limits without losing quality

Text:

  • Objective tone (report what you found, not what you hoped to find)
  • Precise language
  • Appropriate use of scientific terminology
  • Clear references to all figures/tables

üí¨ Section 9: Discussion

‚ñº

Purpose

The discussion is where you interpret your results, connect them to existing knowledge, and explain their broader significance.

Discussion vs. Results-Discussion Combined

Standard Approach: Separate Results and Discussion sections

Alternative: Combined Results-Discussion section

  • Common in some disciplines (especially chemistry)
  • You must discuss with your supervisor before using this format
  • Present each result followed immediately by its interpretation

Required Elements

1. Interpretation of Results

What do your results mean?

  • Brief restatement of key findings
  • What they mean in the limited, immediate sense
  • How they correspond to theoretical expectations from your introduction
  • Whether they support or contradict your hypotheses

Example: "The presence of chemical X after our reaction suggests that the mechanism is..."

2. Comparison with Literature

Connect your work to existing research:

  • How do your results compare with previous studies?
  • Do they confirm, contradict, or extend previous findings?
  • Use specific citations to back up your interpretations
  • Contrast your findings with what others have reported
3. Problems and Limitations
‚ö†Ô∏è Important Balance: Don't overstate this

‚úì Do mention:

  • Significant limitations that affect interpretation
  • Unexpected confounding factors
  • Technical challenges that impacted results

‚ùå Don't:

  • Make this a long list of complaints
  • Focus excessively on what went wrong
  • Use as excuse for poor results
  • Dwell on minor inconveniences
4. Broader Implications

What is the wider impact?

  • How does this advance knowledge in your field?
  • Practical applications (if any)
  • Social implications (e.g., "this may reduce medical costs in the future")
  • Theoretical contributions

Example: "We are one step closer to curing bowel cancer"

5. Future Directions

Where does the research go from here?

  • What would be the logical next experiments?
  • What questions remain unanswered?
  • How could your methods be improved or extended?
  • New questions raised by your findings

Conclusion Paragraph (Required)

End your discussion with a one-paragraph conclusion that:

  • Summarizes the most important aspects of your work
  • Reinforces your key findings
  • States your main contribution
  • Leaves the reader with clear understanding of significance

Discussion Tips

  • ‚úì Be analytical, not descriptive (explain why, not just what)
  • ‚úì Support all claims with evidence (your results or literature)
  • ‚úì Be honest about limitations but don't be overly negative
  • ‚úì Think critically about alternative explanations
  • ‚úì Connect everything back to your original research questions

‚ùå Avoid:

  • Simply restating results without interpretation
  • Ignoring results that don't fit your expectations
  • Making unsupported claims
  • Introducing completely new information not mentioned before

ü§î Section 10: Critical Reflection

‚ñº

What Makes This Section Unique

The critical reflection is what differentiates your bachelor thesis from a standard scientific paper or monograph. This is a unique component that you won't find in journal articles.

Purpose

This section is about you and your personal growth during the BTR. It's where you:

  • Reflect on your experiences throughout the research
  • Identify lessons learned
  • Analyze your personal development
  • Discuss what you could have improved

Key Characteristics

It Must Be CRITICAL

This is not a place to only celebrate successes. You should critically analyze aspects you could have improved.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistake: Students often write only positive reflections. Your assessors want to see self-awareness and critical thinking about your own performance.

What to Discuss

Possible Topics for Reflection:

1. Work Ethic & Time Management
  • How effectively did you manage your time?
  • Were you disciplined in your approach?
  • Did you procrastinate? How did it affect your work?
  • Would you change your work schedule?
2. Planning & Organization
  • How well did you plan your research activities?
  • Were your timelines realistic?
  • Did you anticipate challenges?
  • How did you handle unexpected setbacks?
3. Scientific Skills Development
  • What technical skills did you develop?
  • How steep was your learning curve?
  • What would you do differently in learning new techniques?
  • Which skills still need improvement?
4. Communication & Collaboration
  • How effectively did you communicate with your supervisor?
  • Did you ask for help when needed?
  • How did you work within a research group?
  • Could you have collaborated better?
5. Problem-Solving Approach
  • How did you handle challenges?
  • Were you resourceful in finding solutions?
  • Did you give up too easily or persist when you shouldn't have?
  • What problem-solving strategies worked/didn't work?
6. Independence & Responsibility
  • How well did you work independently?
  • Did you take ownership of your project?
  • Did you need more guidance than expected?
  • How proactive were you?
7. Adaptability
  • How did you handle changes to your research plan?
  • Were you flexible when things didn't go as expected?
  • How well did you cope with uncertainty?
8. Academic Writing & Research Skills
  • How did your writing improve throughout the BTR?
  • What aspects of academic writing remain challenging?
  • How effective were you at literature research?
  • What would you improve about your approach to reading/citing literature?

Structure & Length

No fixed length requirement (but be substantial ‚Äì this is an important component)

Suggested Structure:

  1. Brief introduction to your overall BTR experience
  2. 3-4 main areas of critical reflection (choose from topics above)
  3. Specific examples illustrating your points
  4. Lessons learned and how you'll apply them in future
  5. Brief conclusion about your growth

Writing Approach

  • ‚úì Be honest and genuine ‚Äì assessors value authenticity
  • ‚úì Use specific examples from your experience
  • ‚úì Balance self-criticism with recognition of growth
  • ‚úì Be analytical ‚Äì explain why things happened as they did
  • ‚úì Show self-awareness and maturity
  • ‚úì Look forward ‚Äì how will you use these lessons?

‚ùå Avoid:

  • Only positive comments without critical analysis
  • Blaming others (supervisor, equipment, circumstances)
  • Being overly harsh on yourself
  • Vague generalities without specific examples
  • Repeating discussion points from your Discussion section
Remember: This reflection demonstrates your ability to think critically about yourself ‚Äì an essential skill for any researcher or professional. Your assessors want to see that you've learned and grown from this experience, including learning from mistakes.

üìñ Section 11: References

‚ñº

Purpose

Your reference section provides full bibliographic information for all sources cited in your thesis. This allows readers to locate and consult your sources.

Citation Style

Flexible Options:

Unlike the proposal (which required numbered/Vancouver style), the thesis allows flexibility:

  • MSP Standard: APA format
  • But also acceptable:
    • Vancouver (numbered citations) ‚Äì common in math, computer science, engineering, physics
    • Harvard (author-year in parentheses)
    • Any discipline-specific format
CRITICAL: Consult your Research Supervisor for their preference
  • Many fields have strong conventions
  • Your supervisor may have specific requirements
  • Whatever style you choose must be consistent throughout

In-Text Citations

Purpose: Support statements with evidence from literature

Required For:

  • Factual claims from other research
  • Theoretical concepts not your own
  • Methods adapted from other studies
  • Comparative data from literature
  • Any idea that is not originally yours

Format: Depends on your chosen citation style

  • APA: (Author, Year) or Author (Year)
  • Vancouver: [1] or superscript¬π
  • Harvard: (Author Year)

Full Reference List

Location: End of thesis (after Discussion/Critical Reflection, before Appendices)

Organization:

  • Alphabetical by first author (APA, Harvard)
  • Numerical order as cited (Vancouver)
  • Must match in-text citation style

Number of References

No fixed minimum or maximum (unlike proposal which required 15+)

Expect:

  • Likely more than in your proposal
  • Quality over quantity ‚Äì cite what's relevant
  • Comprehensive coverage of your topic
  • Mix of seminal papers and recent work

Using Reviews vs. Primary Literature

Reviews Are Acceptable For:
  • ‚úì Broad statements supported by multiple studies
  • ‚úì Well-established facts confirmed by extensive research
  • ‚úì General background information
  • ‚úì Overview of a research area
Primary Literature Is Required For:
  • ‚úì Specific individual research findings
  • ‚úì Particular methodological details
  • ‚úì Specific data or results from one study
  • ‚úì Novel or controversial claims
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Rule: It is NOT acceptable to cite references you have not read
  • If you learned about a paper from a review, read the original before citing it
  • "Cited in..." or "as described by..." indicates you didn't read the original ‚Äì avoid this
  • Reading the abstract alone is not sufficient

Reference Format Guidelines

Key Elements (vary by style):

  • Author(s) names
  • Publication year
  • Article/chapter title
  • Journal/book title
  • Volume and issue numbers
  • Page numbers
  • DOI or URL (if applicable)

Typical Journal Article Format (APA):

Author, A. A., Author, B. B., & Author, C. C. (Year). Title of article. Title of Journal, Volume(Issue), page-page. https://doi.org/xxxx

Typical Book Format (APA):

Author, A. A. (Year). Title of book (Edition). Publisher.

Formatting Tips

  • ‚úì Be consistent in format throughout
  • ‚úì Double-check all citation details
  • ‚úì Use citation management software (EndNote free with UM license, Zotero, Mendeley)
  • ‚úì Proofread carefully ‚Äì many marks are lost due to reference errors
  • ‚úì Verify all DOIs/URLs are working and correct
  • ‚úì Check journal abbreviations if using Vancouver style
Common Errors to Avoid:
  • ‚ùå Inconsistent formatting between references
  • ‚ùå Missing information (volume, pages, DOI)
  • ‚ùå Incorrect author names or initials
  • ‚ùå Wrong publication years
  • ‚ùå Mismatched in-text citations and reference list
  • ‚ùå References in list that aren't cited in text
  • ‚ùå In-text citations without corresponding reference
Online Resources - Need help with formatting?
  • Purdue OWL (excellent citation guides for all styles)
  • RefMe or Citation Machine (automatic formatting tools)
  • Your university library website
  • Ask your supervisor for examples from your field

‚ö†Ô∏è Section 14: Academic Integrity & Plagiarism

‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Zero Tolerance for Plagiarism

Your thesis must be:

  • Original
  • Written entirely by you
  • Properly cited and referenced

Consequences of Plagiarism

Result: AUTOMATIC FAILING GRADE

  • Failing grade for the entire written thesis component
  • Handled according to MSP Rules and Regulations
  • May affect your entire BTR outcome and academic record
  • Could delay graduation
  • Academic integrity violation on your permanent record

What Constitutes Plagiarism?

Violations include (but are not limited to):

1. Copying from Previous Theses
  • Copying all or part of another student's thesis
  • Even with slight modifications
  • Exception: Copying from YOUR OWN proposal into your introduction IS allowed
2. Failure to Cite Properly
  • Not including references for borrowed ideas
  • Not citing references correctly in text
  • Missing reference list entries
  • Paraphrasing without citation
3. Internet Plagiarism
  • Copying text from websites without attribution
  • Paraphrasing online sources without citation
  • Using Wikipedia or similar sources without proper acknowledgment
4. AI and Text Generators
  • Using ChatGPT, AI writing tools, or text generators
  • Any technology that negates your intellectual contribution
  • Even if you edit the generated text afterward
5. Ghostwriting
  • Having someone else write any part of your thesis
  • Paying for writing services
  • Using "thesis editing" services that rewrite your work
6. Citation Fraud
  • Citing sources you have not actually read
  • Listing references that don't support your statements
  • Fabricating citations
7. Self-Plagiarism Without Proper Citation
  • Exception: You may copy from YOUR proposal for your introduction
  • Other previous work must be properly cited if reused

How to Avoid Plagiarism

  • ‚úì Read all sources yourself ‚Äì never cite papers you haven't read
  • ‚úì Take careful notes while reading, keeping track of what's your thought vs. the source
  • ‚úì Use proper citations for every borrowed idea, even if paraphrased
  • ‚úì Paraphrase in your own words ‚Äì don't just change a few words
  • ‚úì Use quotation marks for any direct quotes (use sparingly in scientific writing)
  • ‚úì Include complete reference list with all cited sources
  • ‚úì Keep original research notes to prove your work process if needed
  • ‚úì Use plagiarism detection tools (Turnitin, etc.) to check your own work before submission
  • ‚úì When in doubt, cite! ‚Äì over-citation is better than under-citation
The One Exception: Using Your Proposal

You ARE allowed to copy parts of your own research proposal for your thesis introduction.

  • ‚úì This is the only situation where you can reuse previous written work without citation
  • ‚úì You should revise and improve based on feedback received
  • ‚úì Expand sections that were too brief in the proposal
  • ‚úì This applies only to your introduction section

What If I'm Unsure?

Questions about whether something needs citation?

  • Always err on the side of citing
  • Ask your Research Supervisor
  • Contact BTR Administrator: msp-btr@maastrichtuniversity.nl
  • Consult UM Library academic integrity resources

Detection Methods

Be aware:

  • All theses are checked for plagiarism using detection software
  • Supervisors and Internal Advisors are trained to identify plagiarism
  • Comparison with previous years' theses
  • Internet source matching
  • AI-generated text detection tools
Don't risk your degree ‚Äì do your own work and cite properly!
