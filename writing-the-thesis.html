Where to Submit

‚ö†Ô∏è ONLY via Collent

No other submission method is accepted:

  • Canvas or email submissions will NOT be counted
  • Your thesis will be considered missing if not submitted via Collent
  • Make sure you understand the Collent platform before deadline day

üîó Collent Platform

File Size Limit: 25 MB Maximum

How to Achieve This:

  • Compress all images to maximum 300 dpi resolution
  • Use appropriate image formats (JPEG for photos, PNG for diagrams)
  • Check file size before final submission

Special Circumstances

Option 1: Heavy Images Throughout Thesis

  • Save a compressed version (under 25 MB) for Collent upload
  • Include an external download link (SURFdrive, Google Drive, etc.) on your title page
  • Clearly indicate: "Full resolution version available at: [link]"
  • CRITICAL: Explicitly inform both your Research Supervisor and Internal Advisor about the external link

Option 2: Heavy Images Only in Appendix

  • Create an electronic appendix hosted externally
  • Provide link at the beginning of your appendix section
  • Note: "High-resolution appendix available at: [link]"
  • This can be consulted separately by assessors

File Naming Convention

Format:

LastName_StudentID_Thesis.pdf

Example: Jeurissen_i6123456_Thesis.pdf

üèóÔ∏è Complete Thesis Structure

‚ñº

Your thesis should include all of the following sections in order:

1. Title Page

‚úì Required Elements

  • Thesis Title
  • Student Name
  • Student ID (i6XXXXXX)
  • Research Supervisor Name
  • Word Count Declaration

Optional Elements

  • Internal Advisor name
  • Submission date
  • Institution/department
  • University logos
  • External link (if applicable)

2. Abstract (Maximum 200 words)

Must Include:
  • Brief background/context
  • Your research question or aim
  • Methods used (very briefly)
  • Key results
  • Main conclusions
  • Significance/implications

Writing Tips

  • ‚úì Write your abstract last (after completing all other sections)
  • ‚úì Be concise but comprehensive
  • ‚úì Use clear, direct language
  • ‚úì Avoid jargon where possible
  • ‚úì Make every word count ‚Äì you only have 200!
  • ‚ùå Do NOT include citations/references
  • ‚ùå Do NOT include tables or figures

3. Introduction

Good News:

Your introduction will likely be based on your research proposal.

  • ‚úì This is NOT plagiarism ‚Äì you are allowed to copy parts of your proposal
  • ‚úì Revise and improve based on feedback received
  • ‚úì Expand sections that were too brief in the proposal
  • ‚úì Update with relevant literature published since your proposal

Required Elements:

Broader Context ("The Story So Far")
  • What has been done in this research area?
  • What is the current state of knowledge?
  • Why is this topic important?
Knowledge Gaps
  • Where are the gaps in our current understanding?
  • What questions remain unanswered?
  • Why does this gap matter?
Your Research Focus
  • How does your study address these gaps?
  • What is your specific contribution?
  • Your hypotheses or research questions
Wider Significance
  • Potential impact of your work
  • How it enhances knowledge in the field
  • Broader implications (if applicable)
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Rule:

Do NOT simply cite references you've only read about in other papers

  • You must have read the original sources yourself
  • Citing papers you haven't read will result in downgrading
  • If you found a reference in another paper, go read the original before citing it

4. Methods/Experimental Section

Another scientist should be able to precisely replicate your work based on your description.

Materials

  • All equipment used
  • Chemicals/reagents
  • Software and versions
  • Special preparations

Procedures

  • Step-by-step description
  • Order of operations
  • Timing and conditions
  • Sample sizes
  • Controls used

Statistical Analysis

  • Software used
  • Statistical tests performed
  • Significance levels
  • Data processing methods
Golden Rule:

When in doubt, include more detail rather than less. Your assessors should never have to guess what you did.

5. Results

Present what you found without interpretation. Show your data objectively.

Types of Results Presentation:

  • Figures: Graphs showing trends, patterns, or relationships
  • Tables: Organized data in rows and columns
  • Text Descriptions: Guide readers through your results
  • Statistical Analysis: Report relevant statistics and p-values
What NOT to Include:
  • ‚ùå Interpretation of results (save for Discussion)
  • ‚ùå Raw data (unless specifically requested ‚Äì use appendix)
  • ‚ùå Failed experiments (unless relevant to understanding your final approach)

6. Discussion

Interpret your results, connect them to existing knowledge, and explain their broader significance.

Required Elements:

Interpretation of Results

What do your results mean? How do they correspond to theoretical expectations?

Comparison with Literature

How do your results compare with previous studies? Do they confirm or contradict?

Problems and Limitations

Mention significant limitations, but don't overstate or make excuses.

Broader Implications

How does this advance knowledge? What are the practical applications?

Future Directions

What would be the logical next experiments? What questions remain?

Conclusion Paragraph (Required):

End your discussion with one paragraph that:

  • Summarizes the most important aspects of your work
  • Reinforces your key findings
  • States your main contribution
  • Leaves the reader with clear understanding of significance

7. Critical Reflection

What Makes This Section Unique:

This is what differentiates your bachelor thesis from a standard scientific paper. This section is about YOU and your personal growth during the BTR.

It Must Be CRITICAL

This is not a place to only celebrate successes. You should critically analyze aspects you could have improved.

Possible Topics for Reflection:

‚è∞ Time Management

How effectively did you manage your time? Did you procrastinate?

üìã Planning

Were your timelines realistic? Did you anticipate challenges?

üî¨ Skills Development

What technical skills did you develop? How steep was the learning curve?

üí¨ Communication

How effectively did you communicate with your supervisor?

üß© Problem-Solving

How did you handle challenges? Were you resourceful?

üéØ Independence

How well did you work independently? Were you proactive?

Remember:

This reflection demonstrates your ability to think critically about yourself ‚Äì an essential skill for any researcher or professional. Your assessors want to see that you've learned and grown from this experience, including learning from mistakes.

8. References

Citation Style:

Unlike the proposal, the thesis allows flexibility:

  • MSP Standard: APA format
  • Also acceptable: Vancouver (numbered), Harvard, or discipline-specific format
  • CRITICAL: Consult your Research Supervisor for their preference
  • Whatever style you choose must be consistent throughout
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Rule:

It is NOT acceptable to cite references you have not read

  • If you learned about a paper from a review, read the original before citing it
  • "Cited in..." or "as described by..." indicates you didn't read the original ‚Äì avoid this
  • Reading the abstract alone is not sufficient

Draft Review Opportunity

You are allowed ONE draft review from your Research Supervisor.

Options:

Option 1: One Complete Draft

Submit full thesis draft before final version, receive comprehensive feedback, revise and submit final version

Option 2: Section-by-Section Feedback

Submit each section as completed, receive feedback individually, build final thesis from reviewed sections

Option 3: Supervisor-Specific Process

Some supervisors may have their own preferred approach. Clarify expectations early!

‚ö†Ô∏è Your Responsibility:
  • Initiate the draft review process with your supervisor

  • Don't wait until the last minute to request feedback

If Feedback Is Delayed

  2. Check your email (including spam/junk folders)

  4. Contact your Internal Advisor

‚öñÔ∏è Academic Integrity & Plagiarism

‚ñº
‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Zero Tolerance for Plagiarism

Result: AUTOMATIC FAILING GRADE

  • Failing grade for the entire written thesis component
  • Handled according to MSP Rules and Regulations
  • May affect your entire BTR outcome and academic record
  • Could delay graduation
  • Academic integrity violation on your permanent record

What Constitutes Plagiarism?

‚ùå Copying Theses

Copying all or part of another student's thesis, even with slight modifications

‚ùå Failure to Cite

Not including references for borrowed ideas or paraphrasing without citation

‚ùå Internet Plagiarism

Copying text from websites without attribution or proper acknowledgment

‚ùå AI & Text Generators

Using ChatGPT, AI writing tools, or any technology that negates your intellectual contribution

‚ùå Ghostwriting

Having someone else write any part of your thesis or using "editing" services that rewrite

‚ùå Citation Fraud

Citing sources you haven't read, fabricating citations, or listing irrelevant references

The One Exception: Using Your Proposal

You ARE allowed to copy parts of your own research proposal for your thesis introduction.

  • ‚úì This is the only situation where you can reuse previous written work without citation
  • ‚úì You should revise and improve based on feedback received
  • ‚úì Expand sections that were too brief in the proposal
  • ‚úì This applies only to your introduction section

How to Avoid Plagiarism

  • ‚úì Read all sources yourself ‚Äì never cite papers you haven't read
  • ‚úì Take careful notes while reading, keeping track of what's your thought vs. the source
  • ‚úì Use proper citations for every borrowed idea, even if paraphrased
  • ‚úì Paraphrase in your own words ‚Äì don't just change a few words
  • ‚úì Use quotation marks for any direct quotes (use sparingly in scientific writing)
  • ‚úì Include complete reference list with all cited sources
  • ‚úì Keep original research notes to prove your work process if needed
  • ‚úì Use plagiarism detection tools to check your own work before submission
  • ‚úì When in doubt, cite! ‚Äì over-citation is better than under-citation

Detection Methods

Be Aware:
  • All theses are checked for plagiarism using detection software
  • Supervisors and Internal Advisors are trained to identify plagiarism
  • Comparison with previous years' theses
  • Internet source matching
  • AI-generated text detection tools

Don't risk your degree ‚Äì do your own work and cite properly!
