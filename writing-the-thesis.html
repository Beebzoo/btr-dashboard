BTR Written Thesis Guidelines

Your comprehensive guide to writing your final thesis

Your written thesis is the fullest account of your BTR work, presenting your results, conclusions, and personal reflection. This document constitutes 25% of your overall BTR grade and is assessed by both your Research Supervisor and Internal Advisor.

You can begin writing immediately after receiving proposal feedback, and many sections can be written as your work progresses. Use this guide to understand what's expected and how to produce a high-quality academic thesis.

When Can I Start Writing?

You can begin writing your thesis directly after receiving feedback on your proposal. Many parts can be written as your work progresses:

  • Introduction: Based on your proposal (revised according to feedback)
  • Methods/Experimental Section: Write as you conduct your research
  • Results: Document as data is collected
  • Analysis & Discussion: Typically completed in final weeks
  • Critical Reflection: Write during final weeks

Deadline

The written thesis is due five weeks before graduation.

Your exact deadline is provided in the personalized calendar you received at the start of your BTR.

‚ö†Ô∏è Plan Accordingly: Set aside several weeks at the end of your research for:
  • Analysis of results
  • Writing your discussion and conclusions
  • Completing your critical reflection
  • Proofreading and formatting
  • Final submission preparations

How to Achieve This:

  • Compress all images to maximum 300 dpi resolution
  • Use appropriate image formats (JPEG for photos, PNG for diagrams)
  • Check file size before final submission

Special Circumstances

Option 1: Heavy Images Throughout Thesis

  • Save a compressed version (under 25 MB) for Collent upload
  • Include an external download link (SURFdrive, Google Drive, etc.) on your title page
  • Clearly indicate: "Full resolution version available at: [link]"
  • CRITICAL: Explicitly inform both your Research Supervisor and Internal Advisor about the external link

Option 2: Heavy Images Only in Appendix

  • Create an electronic appendix hosted externally
  • Provide link at the beginning of your appendix section
  • Note: "High-resolution appendix available at: [link]"
  • This can be consulted separately by assessors

File Naming Convention

Use the format: LastName_StudentID_Thesis.pdf

Example: Jeurissen_i6123456_Thesis.pdf

  • Use appropriate spacing
  • Ensure all text is clearly readable
  • Follow any supervisor-specific preferences

Abstract

What is an Abstract?

The abstract provides a brief but complete overview of your entire work. It should be placed at the start of your thesis (after title page, before main text).

Requirements

Maximum Length: 200 words

Must Include:

  • Brief background/context
  • Your research question or aim
  • Methods used (very briefly)
  • Key results
  • Main conclusions
  • Significance/implications

Should NOT Include:

  • Citations/references
  • Detailed methodology
  • Extensive background information
  • Tables or figures

Purpose

The abstract allows readers to quickly understand:

  • What you studied
  • How you studied it
  • What you found
  • Why it matters

Someone reading only your abstract should understand the essence of your entire thesis.

Writing Tips:
  • ‚úì Write your abstract last (after completing all other sections)
  • ‚úì Be concise but comprehensive
  • ‚úì Use clear, direct language
  • ‚úì Avoid jargon where possible
  • ‚úì Make every word count ‚Äì you only have 200!
