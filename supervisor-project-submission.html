What Makes a Good BTR Project?

We welcome a wide variety of research projects! While BTR projects should follow the scientific method, we understand that research takes many forms. Here are some general guidelines (not strict requirements):

Core Elements (Flexible)

  • Research question: A clear scientific question the student will investigate
  • Independent work: Opportunities for the student to work autonomously (with your guidance)
  • Scientific methodology: Appropriate approaches for collecting and analyzing data
  • Learning opportunity: New skills, techniques, or knowledge the student will gain
  • Achievable scope: Can be reasonably completed in 5 months (full-time work)

üí° Don't Worry About Perfect Fit

Projects don't need to meet every criterion perfectly! We encourage you to share your ideas even if you're unsure whether they fit. The BTR Office can help determine suitability and work with you to adapt projects as needed.

When in doubt, share it! Many successful BTR projects started as rough ideas that were refined through discussion.

Examples of Successful BTR Projects

Past BTR students have completed projects across diverse fields:

  • Laboratory-based experimental research (biology, chemistry, physics)
  • Computational and modeling projects (bioinformatics, data science)
  • Field research and ecological studies
  • Materials science and engineering projects
  • Interdisciplinary research combining multiple fields
  • Industry-based applied research projects
  • Theoretical and literature-based investigations

‚ö†Ô∏è What Typically Doesn't Qualify

To set appropriate expectations, the following generally don't work as BTR projects:

  • Purely routine laboratory tasks without research questions
  • General internships without a defined research component
  • Service work that doesn't lead to research findings
  • Projects without opportunities for data collection and analysis

If you're unsure about your project, please contact us - we're happy to discuss!

What to Include in Your Project Description

Your description doesn't need to be lengthy, but should give students a clear idea of the project. Consider including:

  • Research topic/question: What will the student investigate?
  • Brief context: Why is this research important or interesting?
  • Methods/approach: What techniques or approaches will be used?
  • Student role: What will the student actually do?
  • Skills gained: What will the student learn?
  • Prerequisites (if any): Required courses or background knowledge
  • Location: Where will the research take place?
  • Timing: When you'd like the student to start (September or February cohort)

‚úèÔ∏è Writing Tips

  • Keep it conversational: Write as if explaining to an interested student
  • Be specific but flexible: Outline the project but note it can be adapted
  • Highlight exciting aspects: What makes this project engaging?
  • Mention support: What guidance/training will you provide?

What Happens After You Submit?

Our Review Process

  1. Receipt Confirmation: We'll confirm receipt of your project within 2-3 business days
  2. Initial Review: The BTR Office reviews your project to ensure it meets BTR requirements
  3. Clarification (if needed): We may contact you with questions or suggestions
  4. Publication: Approved projects are shared with students via Canvas and during BTR information sessions
  5. Student Interest: Students contact you directly if they're interested in your project

Timeline Expectations

  • For September start: Submit projects by June for maximum visibility
  • For February start: Submit projects by November for maximum visibility
  • Ongoing submissions: We accept project submissions year-round

üì¢ How Students Find Your Project

  • Published on MSP Canvas page (accessible to all eligible students)
  • Shared during BTR information sessions
  • Included in email announcements to students
  • Available through BTR Office consultations

What If Students Contact Me?

When students express interest in your project:

  • Initial discussion: Meet (in person or online) to discuss the project and assess mutual fit
  • Student responsibility: Students must complete a "Request to Start" (RTS) form through the BTR Office
  • BTR Office coordination: The BTR Office assigns an Internal Advisor and finalizes arrangements
  • External institutions: Internship agreements must be signed before student can begin
  • Start date: Projects typically begin September 1 or February 2 (unless alternative arrangements made)

üí° Good to Know

You are not obligated to accept every student who expresses interest. It's important to find a good fit between student background/interests and your project requirements. Feel free to interview students and make decisions based on what's best for your research and the student's learning.
