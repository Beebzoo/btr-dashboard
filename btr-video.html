What Makes Your Video Different

This is NOT a Research Presentation

Your BTR video is fundamentally different from academic presentations or your thesis defense.

Key Difference:

‚úì BTR Video: Aimed at communicating your research to non-scientists

‚úó Research Presentation: Aimed at scientists in your field

What This Means:

‚ùå Don't:

  • Use complex, difficult-to-explain scientific terms
  • Assume audience has specialized knowledge
  • Focus primarily on technical methodology
  • Record your defense presentation and submit that

‚úì Do:

  • Explain concepts in simple, accessible language
  • Use analogies and examples that anyone can understand
  • Focus on why your research matters to society
  • Make it engaging and relatable

Your Target Audience

Think of your video as being watched by:

  • Your parents or grandparents (unless they're scientists!)
  • A high school student interested in science
  • A policy maker deciding on research funding
  • A journalist writing about scientific advances
  • Anyone curious about science but without specialist training
Your Goal:

Make them understand and care about your research, even without scientific background.

Required Content Elements

Your video must address four key elements. Each is essential and will be assessed.

1. Scientific Importance ‚öóÔ∏è

What to Explain:

  • How does this work increase our knowledge of the world/universe?
  • What gap in scientific understanding does it fill?
  • What didn't we know before that we know now (or will know)?
  • How does it fit into the bigger scientific picture?

Approach:

  • Place your work in the context of broader scientific questions
  • Explain what previous research established
  • Show where the gap was
  • Demonstrate how your work begins to fill that gap

Example Framing:

"Scientists have known X for years, but we didn't understand Y. My research helps us understand Y by..."

2. Societal Importance üåç

What to Explain:

  • What contribution does your work make to a better world?
  • How might it improve people's lives?
  • What are the potential real-world applications?

Possible Impacts:

For Applied Research:

  • Improvements to human health and welfare
  • Environmental benefits
  • Technological advances
  • Economic benefits
  • Solutions to practical problems

For "Blue Sky" Research:

Even pure research without obvious applications needs societal justification:

  • Why does this knowledge matter?
  • Why should society fund this research?
  • What are the potential long-term benefits?
  • How does it contribute to human understanding?
  • Could it lead to unexpected applications?

Be Honest:

If your work is primarily curiosity-driven, explain:

  • Why understanding this is inherently valuable
  • How fundamental research often leads to unexpected breakthroughs
  • Examples of how "useless" research became transformative

3. Ethical Considerations ‚öñÔ∏è

CRITICAL:

There is no piece of science free from ethical considerations.

What to Discuss:

Every research project has ethical dimensions. Your video must address these.

Common Ethical Areas:

Work with Living Subjects:

  • Animal welfare considerations
  • Human participant rights and protections
  • Consent and privacy issues
  • Potential harm or risk
  • Justification for methods used

Data Ethics:

  • How data is collected
  • Privacy and anonymity protections
  • Data storage and security
  • Who has access to data
  • Data sharing practices
  • Long-term data management

Resource Allocation:

  • Is this research the best use of (public) funding?
  • Given limited resources, why prioritize this work?
  • Opportunity costs of research choices

Environmental Impact:

  • Energy consumption
  • Waste production
  • Use of materials or chemicals
  • Long-term environmental effects

Dual-Use Concerns:

  • Could results be misused?
  • Potential negative applications
  • Responsibilities in disseminating findings

Social Justice:

  • Who benefits from this research?
  • Who might be harmed or excluded?
  • Equity considerations

Your Approach:

  • Identify which ethical dimensions apply to your work
  • Address them thoughtfully and honestly
  • Show awareness that research carries responsibilities
  • Demonstrate that you've thought critically about these issues
‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Say: "My research has no ethical considerations"
‚úì Do Say: "The key ethical considerations in my work are..."

4. Clear Structure & Storytelling üìñ

Your video must have:

Opening Statement:

  • Hook the audience immediately
  • State clearly what your research is about
  • Why should they care?

Middle (Main Content):

  • Logically organized sections
  • Clear transitions between ideas
  • Progressive building of understanding
  • Visual aids to support explanations

Conclusion:

  • Summarize key takeaways
  • Reinforce why it matters
  • Leave audience with clear understanding of significance

Storytelling Tips:

  • ‚úì Create a narrative arc (problem ‚Üí investigation ‚Üí findings ‚Üí impact)
  • ‚úì Use the "inverted pyramid" - most important points first
  • ‚úì Build complexity gradually - simple to more detailed
  • ‚úì Connect back to your opening throughout

File Format MP4 (MPEG-4)

  • Properly cropped and formatted

  • Not distracting
  • Professional appearance

Audio Quality

Audio is Critical for Viewer Experience

Poor audio quality will significantly impact your grade and audience engagement.

Requirements

Clear Voice:

  • Speak clearly and at appropriate volume
  • Articulate carefully
  • Vary tone to maintain interest (avoid monotone)
  • Pace yourself - not too fast, not too slow

No Background Noise:

  • Record in quiet environment
  • No traffic sounds
  • No phone notifications
  • No people chatting in background
  • No echo or reverb

Audio-Video Synchronization:

  • Audio must perfectly match video
  • No lag or delay
  • Test before final submission

Recording Tips

Environment:

  • ‚úì Record in quiet room
  • ‚úì Close windows (traffic noise)
  • ‚úì Turn off phones and notifications
  • ‚úì Inform others not to interrupt
  • ‚úì Consider time of day (avoid noisy periods)

Equipment:

  • ‚úì Use external microphone if possible (better than built-in)
  • ‚úì Test audio levels before recording
  • ‚úì Position microphone appropriately (6-12 inches from mouth)
  • ‚úì Record test clip and listen critically

If Audio is Poor:

  • Consider re-recording
  • Use audio editing software to reduce noise (carefully)
  • Ensure any edits don't create unnatural sound

Presentation & Delivery

Your On-Camera Presence Matters

How you present yourself impacts how your message is received.

Appearance

Dress Appropriately:

  • Professional but comfortable
  • Solid colors often work better than patterns
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Avoid checkered patterns (can create visual artifacts on camera)
  • Consider what suits your research topic's tone

Grooming & Posture:

  • Look presentable and professional
  • Good posture - sit or stand up straight
  • Face clearly visible
  • Hair not obscuring face

Body Language & Delivery

Energy Level:

  • Seem relaxed but not too relaxed
  • Maintain high energy levels throughout
  • Show enthusiasm for your work
  • Avoid appearing bored or disinterested

Eye Contact:

  • Look into the camera (not at screen or notes)
  • Creates connection with audience
  • Looking elsewhere appears shifty or uncertain
  • Practice looking at camera lens specifically

Facial Expressions:

  • Appropriate to content
  • Show genuine interest
  • Smile when appropriate
  • Avoid appearing robotic or overly serious

Communication Style

Language:

  • No jargon - explain everything in accessible terms
  • Use examples and analogies to illustrate complex points
  • Stick to the point - don't ramble or digress
  • Be concise and clear

Tone:

  • Consider the style appropriate to your topic
  • Humor can work but be careful with sensitive topics
  • Example: Don't use humor when discussing terminal disease
  • Match tone to subject matter seriousness

Pacing:

  • Not too fast (audience needs time to process)
  • Not too slow (maintains interest)
  • Pause between major points
  • Use pacing to emphasize important information

Visual Elements & Editing

Visual Design Elements

  • Ensures you're visible while showing visuals
  • Keep consistent positioning throughout

Photos & Images:

  • High resolution - not pixelated or distorted
  • Properly sized to fill frame appropriately
  • Clear and relevant to narration
  • Must be copyright-free (see Copyright section below)

Visual Flow:

  • Keep it visually interesting
  • Don't stay on same image/slide for too long
  • Vary visual elements
  • Match visuals to narration
  • Use transitions thoughtfully (not excessive)

Technical Quality Checks

Stability:

  • Video should not be shaky
  • Use tripod or secure camera position
  • No handheld recording unless necessary

Clarity:

  • Image should not be cloudy or blurred
  • Clean camera lens before recording
  • Ensure adequate lighting
  • Check focus

Lighting:

  • Face should be well-lit
  • Avoid backlighting (don't sit in front of window)
  • Even lighting without harsh shadows
  • Natural or artificial light both work if properly used

Copyright & Open Source Materials

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Copyright Compliance

You MUST NOT use copyrighted material in your video.

What You Cannot Use

  • ‚ùå Other people's videos (clips from YouTube, films, TV, etc.)
  • ‚ùå Copyrighted images (stock photos without license, others' photographs)
  • ‚ùå Copyrighted music (commercial songs, background music with rights)
  • ‚ùå Copyrighted graphics (branded logos without permission, illustrations)

Why This Matters

Serious Consequences:

  • Videos may be shared publicly for promotional purposes (if you consent)
  • Other people may see these videos
  • If someone sees copyrighted material you've "stolen," there could be legal trouble
  • University could face copyright infringement claims
  • You could face academic integrity violations

Your Responsibility:

  • Ensure all materials are properly licensed
  • Document sources for all visual elements
  • When in doubt, don't use it

What You CAN Use

‚úì Your own original content

  • Photos you've taken yourself
  • Graphics/diagrams you've created
  • Your own voice and presentation
  • Lab equipment, experiments, etc. you have permission to film

‚úì Open source materials

  • Creative Commons licensed images (check specific CC license)
  • Public domain materials
  • Properly licensed stock photos/videos (free or purchased)
  • University or research group materials you have permission to use

‚úì Properly licensed content

  • Materials with explicit permission from creator
  • Licensed stock resources
  • Open access scientific figures (with proper attribution)

Finding Open Source Materials

Image Sources:

  • Unsplash (free high-quality photos)
  • Pixabay (free images and videos)
  • Pexels (free stock photos)
  • Wikimedia Commons (check licenses)
  • Creative Commons Search

Video Sources:

  • Pexels Videos
  • Pixabay Videos
  • Videvo (free stock footage)

Music Sources (if using background music):

  • YouTube Audio Library
  • Free Music Archive
  • Incompetech (royalty-free music)
  • Always check license terms!
‚ö†Ô∏è Verify License Terms:
  • Some Creative Commons licenses require attribution
  • Some prohibit commercial use (but educational use usually OK)
  • Read license details for each resource
  • Keep records of licenses for materials used

Attribution

If license requires attribution:

  • Include credits at end of video
  • List all sources with proper attribution
  • Format: "Image by [creator] from [source]"

Subtitles & Accessibility

Subtitles - Recommended but Not Required

Benefits of Subtitles:

Accessibility:

  • Essential for deaf or hard-of-hearing viewers
  • Helps non-native English speakers
  • Allows viewing in sound-sensitive environments

Practical Uses:

  • Videos can be watched without sound (public spaces, libraries)
  • Improves comprehension of technical terms
  • Viewers can read along while listening

Professional Quality:

  • Makes video more polished
  • Shows extra effort and consideration
  • Improves overall viewing experience

Creating Subtitles

Options:

1. YouTube Auto-Captions (Free)

  • Upload video to YouTube (can be private/unlisted)
  • YouTube generates automatic captions
  • Download .srt file
  • Edit for accuracy
  • Add to final video

2. Video Editing Software

  • Most editing programs have subtitle features
  • Add text overlays timed to speech
  • Export with burned-in subtitles

3. Online Subtitle Tools

  • Subtitle Edit (free software)
  • Amara (online subtitle editor)
  • Various subtitle generation services

4. Manual Creation

  • Create .srt or .vtt subtitle file
  • Time stamps for each caption
  • Embed in video or provide as separate track

Subtitle Best Practices

If You Include Subtitles:

  • ‚úì Accurate transcription of spoken words
  • ‚úì Properly timed to match speech
  • ‚úì Readable text size and font
  • ‚úì High contrast (white text on dark background or vice versa)
  • ‚úì Appropriate duration - text visible long enough to read
  • ‚úì Include sound descriptions [if relevant] for non-speech sounds
  • ‚úì Proofread carefully - spelling and timing
‚ö†Ô∏è Note: Subtitles are not absolutely necessary, and can be technically difficult

Decision: Weigh benefit vs. time investment

  • For students with experience: Recommended
  • For students without experience: Optional
  • Don't add subtitles if they'll be poorly done (better to have none)

Common Questions & Troubleshooting

Technical Issues

Q: My video file is over 2GB. What do I do?

A: Compress your video using these methods:

  • Export at lower bitrate (5-8 Mbps often sufficient)
  • Reduce resolution if necessary (720p instead of 1080p)
  • Use H.264 codec for efficient compression
  • Check if video editing software has "web" or "online" presets
  • Remove any unnecessary intro/outro credits that add length

Q: How do I convert PowerPoint to MP4?

A: In PowerPoint:

  1. File ‚Üí Export ‚Üí Create a Video
  2. Choose quality (1080p or 720p)
  3. Set timings or record narration
  4. Save as MPEG-4 video

Q: WeTransfer link expired before grading. What now?

A: This is why SURFdrive backup is mandatory:

  • Assessors can access video from SURFdrive
  • Make sure your SURFdrive upload succeeded
  • Contact BTR Office if concerned about access

Content Questions

Q: Can I show faces of lab members in my video?

A: Yes, if you have their permission:

  • Ask lab members beforehand
  • Better to show yourself primarily
  • Consider using photos of people rather than video if consent unclear

Q: How technical can I be in explaining my methods?

A: Keep it accessible:

  • Explain basic principles rather than detailed procedures
  • Use analogies to make technical processes understandable
  • Focus on what you did and why rather than every specific detail
  • Remember: non-scientist audience

Q: What if my research doesn't have obvious societal importance?

A: All research has value to society:

  • Explain how fundamental knowledge advances human understanding
  • Discuss potential long-term applications (even if speculative)
  • Explain why society benefits from funding curiosity-driven research
  • Give examples of "useless" research that became transformative

Q: My research doesn't involve animals or people. What ethical considerations apply?

A: Consider:

  • Data handling and privacy (even for non-human subjects)
  • Environmental impact of your research
  • Resource allocation - why this research over other priorities?
  • Potential misuse of findings
  • Who benefits and who might be harmed?

Remember: There is no research free from ethical considerations

Q: Can I use humor in my video?

A: Yes, but use judgment:

  • Appropriate for most topics if tasteful
  • Avoid for serious topics (terminal illness, human suffering, etc.)
  • Keep it light and relevant
  • Don't let humor undermine the seriousness of your work

Peer Review Questions

Q: When will I know which video to review?

A:

  • Assignments made after submission deadline passes
  • Check Collent and email for notification
  • Usually same day as submission deadline

Q: What if the peer video I'm assigned is terrible?

A:

  • Still provide constructive feedback
  • Use grading rubric honestly but fairly
  • Identify specific areas for improvement
  • Remember this is a learning experience for your peer
  • Focus on helping them understand how to improve

Q: Can I request extension for peer review?

A:

  • Generally no - this is why we recommend same-day completion
  • Strict 1.0 point penalty for non-submission
  • Complete it promptly after assignment
  • Contact BTR Office only in exceptional circumstances
